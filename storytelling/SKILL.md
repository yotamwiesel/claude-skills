# Storytelling for Short-Form Content

Structure any narrative content using proven storytelling frameworks. The goal is content people watch to the end because they need to know what happens next - not because they're learning facts.

## The Core Rule

The South Park Rule: between every beat in the story, the connector must be "but" or "therefore" - never "and then." If events connect with "and then," the story is a list, not a narrative. Every beat must be a consequence of or complication from the previous one.

Test: read the body of any script. Replace each transition with "and then." If it still makes sense, the story has no tension. Rewrite.

## Before Writing

Three steps before any draft:

1. **Know your punchline.** Write the last line first. Every sentence from the first to the last must lead to that single moment. If you discover the point while writing, you're meandering.
2. **What ONE emotion should the viewer feel?** Relief, disbelief, envy, curiosity, pride. Pick one. Shape everything around it.
3. **What framework fits?** Read `references/frameworks.md` to select the right structure. Default to ABT when unsure.

### "How I" Not "How To"

Frame everything as personal experience, not instruction. "How To" is preachy and triggers defensiveness. "How I" is your truth - no one can challenge what you lived through.

Bad: "3 ways to organize your inbox"
Good: "I had 5,445 unread emails. Yesterday I wrote one sentence and walked away."

## The ABT Framework (Default)

For any "I did X and here's what happened" content:

**AND** (the world before) - Establish the pain. Be specific and relatable. The viewer must see themselves here. Not "I had a problem" but "every time I opened X, I'd close it after a second because I didn't know where to start."

**BUT** (the turning point) - The epiphany moment, not a feature introduction. Not "I opened the tool" but "then I thought - wait, why am I not just asking it to do this?" The viewer experiences the aha moment with the creator.

**THEREFORE** (the consequence chain) - Each result leads to the next, with escalating stakes:
- THEREFORE #1: The immediate result (it worked)
- THEREFORE #2: The surprising detail that raises stakes (it did something unexpected)
- THEREFORE #3: The permanent change (now everything is different)

## The Neurochemistry Tripod (Angel's Cocktail)

Every story must trigger at least 2 of these 3 brain chemicals. If it only has one, the emotional layer is missing.

- **Dopamine** (suspense, cliffhangers, curiosity gaps) - gives focus, motivation, memory. "What happens next?" is dopamine.
- **Oxytocin** (empathy, vulnerability, human connection) - gives trust, bonding, generosity. A relatable struggle or a damaging admission is oxytocin.
- **Endorphins** (humor, surprise, delight) - gives creativity, relaxation, openness. A laugh resets attention.

Diagnostic: read your draft. Mark each beat D (dopamine), O (oxytocin), or E (endorphin). If you only see D's, add a vulnerable moment. If you only see O's, add a cliffhanger. A flat chemical profile = a flat story.

### Damaging Admissions

Leading with a flaw or mistake is the fastest way to build trust. "I wasted 6 months doing it wrong" before "here's what actually works" makes the solution credible. Without the admission, you're just another guru.

## The 2+2 Theory (Pixar)

Never hand the audience "4." Give them "2+2" and let them deduce the conclusion themselves. People are natural problem-solvers - when they figure it out, they feel smart and invested.

Bad: "Claude Code saves you 3 hours a day"
Good: "I used to spend 3 hours on this. Yesterday I opened Claude Code and it was done before my coffee got cold."

The audience concludes "that saves 3 hours" on their own. They own the insight. It sticks.

## Escalation (Staircasing)

Every section must operate at a higher energy or stakes level than the previous one. If two consecutive beats feel the same, cut one.

Bad: "he labeled emails" -> "he archived emails" -> "he organized labels" (same level)
Good: "he sorted everything" -> "the spam? he asked before touching it" -> "now he does it every week without being told" (each beat surprises)

## Retention Techniques

Read `references/retention-techniques.md` for the full list. The essentials:

- **Open loops**: Main question opens in hook, closes at end. Drop micro-loops throughout the body.
- **Pattern interrupts**: Change something (visual, tone, pace) every 7-10 seconds. Static middles kill watch time.
- **Strategic bridges**: Never flat transitions. "אבל הדבר שהרג אותי?" beats "ואז עוד משהו."
- **One idea per video**: 60 seconds = 125-150 words maximum. Two ideas = two videos.

## Tiny Moments > Grand Stories

The most powerful stories aren't epic adventures - they're "dander in the wind." Small, specific, relatable moments where your heart moved or your perspective changed. A child saving pocket money for a stuffed animal. A secret about childhood hunger. Everyone has felt these things.

Grand stories ("I almost died") are actually less engaging because most people can't relate. Tiny stories connect universally.

## The Anti-Feature-List Rule

The most common failure mode: a feature list with narrative paint.

Feature list (bad): "He entered Gmail, pulled all the emails, classified each one into categories, applied labels, and archived them."

Story (good): "I wrote one sentence and walked away. Came back and he's in the middle of it - going through everything, figuring out what each email is, putting it where it belongs. And the spam? He didn't delete a single one without asking me first."

The difference: the story has a human watching, reacting, being surprised. The feature list has a machine executing steps.

## Applying the Humanizer

After writing any narrative content, run through the humanizer lens:

1. Read it aloud. Does it sound like someone telling a friend, or presenting at a conference?
2. Check for "and then" chains - rewrite with "but" and "therefore"
3. Check for same-energy beats - cut or escalate
4. Check for feature-list-as-story - add the human reaction layer
5. Check for AI vocabulary (see humanizer skill for the full list)

## Format-Specific Notes

### Instagram Reels (30-60 sec)

- Hook (0-3s): Result or number that stops the scroll. Use signaling colors (red, orange, yellow) and high contrast.
- Body: ABT with 2-3 escalating THEREFORE beats
- Loop closure: One sentence answering the hook's implicit question
- CTA: Directly tied to content
- **6 objects max on screen** - more than 6 costs the brain 500% more energy. It gives up and scrolls.
- **No text-speech redundancy** - if the text on screen says exactly what the voice says, retention drops to near zero. Text should enhance, not duplicate.

### Ad Copy

- Use PAS (Problem, Agitate, Solve) or Epiphany Bridge from `references/frameworks.md`
- The "agitate" step is where emotion lives - twist the knife before offering relief

### Launch Messages / WhatsApp

- Use Story Spine (Pixar) for longer narratives
- "Because of that" chain creates inevitability - the product feels like a natural consequence, not a pitch

### Case Studies / Testimonials

- Use SOAR (Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result) from `references/frameworks.md`
- Or Before-After-Bridge with the "After" shown first

## Quality Check

Before delivering any narrative content:

1. Can "and then" be inserted between every beat? If yes, rewrite.
2. Does each section escalate stakes? If two feel the same, cut one.
3. Is there a human in the story reacting to events? If just a machine doing things, add the person.
4. Exactly ONE idea? If two, split into two pieces.
5. Does the hook's promise get fulfilled in the body?
6. Read aloud - does it sound like telling a friend at a bar, or reading a press release?
7. **Neurochemistry check**: mark each beat D/O/E. At least 2 of 3 chemicals present? If all dopamine, add a vulnerable human moment (oxytocin). If all empathy, add a curiosity gap (dopamine).
8. **2+2 check**: are you handing the audience "4" anywhere? Rewrite to show the evidence and let them conclude.
9. **"How I" check**: does any section slip into "How To" / preachy mode? Reframe to personal experience.
10. **Damaging admission**: is there at least one moment of honest vulnerability before the payoff?
